Understanding Preterism

Exploring the biblical perspective that many prophecies were fulfilled in the first century

What is Preterism?

Preterism is a Christian eschatological view that interprets many biblical prophecies as having been fulfilled in the past, particularly during the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 AD.

The Preterist Perspective

The term "preterism" comes from the Latin word "praeteritus," meaning "gone by" or "past." Preterists believe that Jesus' prophecies about the "end times" were primarily referring to the end of the Old Covenant age, which culminated with the destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple in 70 AD.

This interpretation emphasizes the historical context of biblical prophecy and the first-century fulfillment of many passages that are often viewed as referring to future events.

Types of Preterism

P

Partial Preterism

Partial preterists believe that many prophecies, including those about the Second Coming in judgment upon Jerusalem, were fulfilled in 70 AD, but maintain that the final resurrection and final judgment are still future.

  • • Olivet Discourse fulfilled in 70 AD
  • • Some aspects of Revelation fulfilled
  • • Future bodily resurrection
  • • Future final judgment
F

Full Preterism

Full preterists (also called hyper-preterists) believe that all biblical prophecies, including the Second Coming, resurrection, and final judgment, were fulfilled by 70 AD.

  • • All prophecy fulfilled by 70 AD
  • • Spiritual resurrection view
  • • Past judgment complete
  • • Present eternal state

Key Scriptures

Matthew 24:34

"Truly I tell you, this generation will certainly not pass away until all these things have happened."

Often cited as evidence for first-century fulfillment of Jesus' prophecies.

Matthew 16:28

"Truly I tell you, some who are standing here will not taste death before they see the Son of Man coming in his kingdom."

Suggests an imminent fulfillment within the disciples' lifetime.

Revelation 1:1

"The revelation from Jesus Christ, which God gave him to show his servants what must soon take place."

The word "soon" suggests nearness in time for the original audience.

Hebrews 8:13

"By calling this covenant 'new,' he has made the first one obsolete; and what is obsolete and outdated will soon disappear."

Indicates the Old Covenant was in the process of passing away.

1 Peter 4:7

"The end of all things is near. Therefore be alert and of sober mind so that you may pray."

Peter's audience understood "the end" to be imminent.

James 5:8-9

"You too, be patient and stand firm, because the Lord's coming is near... The Judge is standing at the door!"

James viewed the Lord's coming as imminent to his readers.
